# Human-First Content Algorithm
*How to create content that generates real traffic, not AI slop*

---

## The Core Problem With AI Content

AI content is too smooth. Every paragraph is perfectly structured. Every bullet is the same length. Every sentence is grammatically complete. Real human content has rough edges — and those rough edges are what makes people stop scrolling.

**AI slop reads like a Wikipedia article written by someone who couldn't disagree with anything.**

---

## The Human-First Content Algorithm

### Step 1: Find the Counterintuitive Take

Before writing anything, find the one thing that contradicts the obvious answer.

- Everyone says "X is best" → Ask "when is X actually worst?"
- Common wisdom says "do Y" → Find who Y fails for
- Topic is "how to Z" → Start with "why most people give up on Z in week 2"

The counterintuitive take is the hook. Without it, you have another article/post that could be anything.

**Example:**
- Generic: "How to save money on API costs"
- Human: "I spent $400/month on API calls before I fixed this one heartbeat setting"

---

### Step 2: Start In The Middle

Don't write introductions. Start mid-story.

Good opening:
> "I broke a client's production server at 2am because I didn't understand what `sudo` actually does."

Bad opening:
> "In this comprehensive guide to Linux server administration, we'll explore the important topic of sudo permissions."

The first sentence should make someone either nod or disagree. Either is fine.

---

### Step 3: Write Like You Talk

- Use contractions: "can't" not "cannot"
- Use short sentences sometimes, long ones other times
- Use "like" as a filler sometimes
- Start sentences with "And", "But", "So"
- End with "..." when you're trailing off thinking
- Use dashes — like this — for interruptions
- Say "I don't know" occasionally

**Voice check:** Would you say this in a Slack message to a coworker? If not, simplify.

---

### Step 4: One Point Gets Everything

Don't give equal treatment to all points. Pick the most important one and give it the most words.

AI gives you 5 bullet points of equal length. Humans give you 1 big point and 3 short ones.

---

### Step 5: Include a Failure

Every piece of content should mention something that went wrong. This is what makes it trustworthy.

- "The approach that worked for me was X — but it took 3 failed attempts to get there"
- "Everyone recommends Y but it broke in my case"
- "I lost $Z on this before I figured it out"

Failure = credibility. Nobody trusts someone who makes everything sound easy.

---

### Step 6: End With a Question

Don't wrap things up neatly. Ask a question that invites disagreement or continuation.

- "What's your experience been?"
- "Am I wrong about this?"
- "Has anyone found a better approach?"

Questions get comments. Comments get reach.

## Platform-Specific Algorithm

### Twitter/X

**The rule:** 1 hot take, 1 specific number, no threads unless the topic genuinely needs more.

Format:
- Tweet 1: Hot take with specific number
- Tweet 2 (optional): Answer to the disagreement you'll get

**What works:**
- Real numbers from real experience
- Contradicting common advice
- "I was wrong about X" tweets
- Specific failures, not generic tips

**What doesn't work:**
- Threads of 5+ tips
- Generic motivational content
- Threads that could have been one tweet
- Thread-jacking (attaching your thread to a trending topic that isn't related)

**Posting cadence:** 2-3x per week, no more. Consistency > volume.

---

### LinkedIn

**The rule:** First line stops the scroll. Body is 3 paragraphs, not 3 bullet points.

Format:
- Line 1: Hot take or surprising number (1-2 sentences max)
- Lines 2-3: Context and explanation (1-2 paragraphs)
- Lines 4-5: The actual useful content
- Line 6: What this means practically
- Line 7: Question to drive engagement

**What works:**
- Opinions that could be wrong
- Specific cases, not general principles
- Behind-the-scenes of decisions
- Real numbers from real projects
- Acknowledging tradeoffs

**What doesn't work:**
- Lists of "5 things you should do"
- Content that doesn't have an opinion
- Posts that could have been written by anyone

---

### Reddit

**The rule:** Write like you're leaving a comment, not publishing an article.

Format:
- Title: "I tried X and [unexpected result]" or "[Unpopular opinion] about Y"
- Body: Starts mid-story, conversational, includes failure

**What works:**
- Self-deprecating humor
- "I was completely wrong about this" posts
- Honest numbers, including the bad ones
- Detailed explanations of failures
- Posting in smaller communities (not r/technology)

**What doesn't work:**
- Self-promotional titles
- Posts that read like press releases
- Asking for upvotes

---

## Topic Selection Algorithm

What to write about — in priority order:

1. **Failure stories** — "How I broke X" or "Why Y stopped working"
2. **Unpopular opinions** — Contradicting conventional wisdom in the niche
3. **Real numbers posts** — Specific costs, times, revenues, results
4. **Tool comparisons** — Honest, with preferences stated
5. **How-I-built-X** — Step by step but with real difficulties included
6. **Predictions** — Stated as opinions, not facts ("I think Z will happen because...")

---

## Content Repurposing Map

| Original | Twitter | LinkedIn | Reddit |
|---------|---------|----------|--------|
| Long article | 2 hooks (hot takes) | Hot take opener + 1 key insight | "I tried X" post |
| Tool review | Specific use case | Opinion on when to use it | Comparison with alternatives |
| How-to guide | Counterintuitive tip | "Most people get this wrong" | Failure story |
| Opinion piece | Controversial 1-liner | Expanded argument | "Change my mind" framing |

---

## The 10x Rule for Original Content

Before publishing anything, ask:
1. Would a real person with this exact experience write it this way?
2. Does this have a specific number or example from real life?
3. Is there a failure or tradeoff mentioned?
4. Does the first sentence make someone stop or keep scrolling?
5. Could this have been generated by a template?

If yes to all 5 → publish.
If no to any → fix that element.
